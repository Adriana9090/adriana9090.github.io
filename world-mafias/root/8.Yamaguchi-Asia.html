Yamaguchi-gumi

History The Sixth Yamaguchi-gumi is Japan's largest yakuza (Japanese transnational crime syndicates) organization. It is named after its founder Harukichi Yamaguchi. Its origins can be traced back to a loose labor union for dockworkers in Kobe before World War II. It is one of the largest criminal organizations in the world. According to the National Police Agency, it had 20,400 active members and 18,600 associated members in 2007. It is, by far, the largest of the bōryokudan groups, and its membership encompasses roughly 45% of the 86,300 yakuza in the Japanese underworld. Leading members of the Yamaguchi-gumi number 102 people in total; 1 kumichō (boss), 15 shatei (younger brother) and 86 wakachū (child). The Yamaguchi-gumi has its headquarters in Kobe, but it operates all across Japan and has overseas operations in the rest of Asia and the United States. Its current kumichō (Boss), Shinobu Tsukasa, has declared an expansionist policy—even making inroads into Tokyo, traditionally not Yamaguchi turf. They also have multiple groups working overseas. After 100 years in business, the Yamaguchi-gumi split apart into two groups in 2015: the original organization, and the Kobe Yamaguchi-gumi. In 2017 there was a third split, with Yoshinori Oda, an underboss of the Kobe Yamaguchi-gumi, creating the Ninkyo (Dantai) Yamaguchi-gumi.

Key Facts

  • Founded: 1915
  • Founding Location: Kobe, Japan
  • Years active: 1915-present
  • Criminal Activities: Money laundry, arms trafficking, drug trafficking, contract killing, Internet pornography, mortgage fraud, securities fraud, prostitution
  • Rivals: Kobe Yamaguchi-gumi

Leaders


Shinobu Tsukasa, Harukichi Yamaguchi, Noboru Yamaguchi and Kazuo Taoka
